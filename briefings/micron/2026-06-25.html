💽 Micron Earnings — Thu Jun 25, 2026

Micron’s latest earnings were less a normal memory-cycle beat than a signal that AI memory has become a contracted, high-margin infrastructure layer.

🟦 Earnings Shock — Confirmed

  • Fiscal Q3 2026 results — Jun 24, confirmed: Micron reported $41.46bn revenue for the quarter ended May 28, 2026, up from $23.86bn in FQ2 and $9.30bn a year earlier. GAAP net income was $28.24bn / $24.67 diluted EPS; non-GAAP net income was $28.86bn / $25.11 diluted EPS.
  • Margins changed the quality of the story: GAAP gross margin reached 84.6% versus 74.4% in FQ2 and 37.7% a year ago; GAAP operating margin was 80.4%. That is not just cyclical recovery — it points to unusually tight supply/demand and premium AI-memory mix.
  • Cash generation was enormous: operating cash flow was $25.39bn, and adjusted free cash flow was $18.30bn after $7.1bn of net capital expenditures. Micron ended the quarter with $30.2bn of cash, marketable investments, and restricted cash.
  • Market read-through: Yahoo Finance’s chart endpoint showed MU closing at $1,213.56 on Jun 25 versus $1,048.51 the prior session, a roughly 15.7% one-day gain. Treat that as a market-reaction marker, not a valuation call.

🟩 Guidance — The Bar Moved Higher

  • FQ4 revenue guide: Micron guided to $50.0bn ± $1.0bn revenue for fiscal Q4 2026.
  • Margin guide: gross margin is guided at ~86%, which implies the company expects tightness and premium mix to persist into the next quarter rather than fade immediately after the Q3 print.
  • EPS guide: GAAP diluted EPS guide is $30.73 ± $1.00; non-GAAP diluted EPS guide is $31.00 ± $1.00, based on roughly 1.15bn diluted shares.
  • Why this matters: the guidance makes Micron less of a “memory spot-price rebound” story and more of an AI-infrastructure supplier story — as long as customer commitments and HBM execution hold.

🟪 AI Memory / Product Roadmap

  • HBM4 is the headline: Micron said HBM4, built on 1-beta DRAM, is in high-volume shipments for its lead customer’s platform, with qualification samples shipped to multiple end customers.
  • HBM4E is already on deck: Micron said HBM4E, built on 1-gamma DRAM, is in development with volume production expected in calendar 2027. That keeps the investor focus on multi-generation AI accelerator attach, not only the current quarter.
  • Server memory depth is broadening: Micron shipped qualification samples of 256GB DDR5 RDIMMs built on 1-gamma DRAM and advanced 3D die stacking to key server ecosystem enablers.
  • Storage is not dead weight: G9-based PCIe Gen6 high-performance SSDs are in high-volume production, and Micron began shipments of 245TB QLC SSDs. The AI-memory story is DRAM/HBM-led, but high-capacity storage remains part of the data-center platform bundle.

🟧 Segment Mix — AI Is Pulling More Than One Lever

  • Cloud Memory Business Unit: revenue was $13.77bn, with 83% gross margin and 78% operating margin. Micron defines this unit as hyperscale cloud memory plus HBM for all data-center customers.
  • Core Data Center Business Unit: revenue was $11.52bn, with 87% gross margin and 83% operating margin. This covers mid-tier cloud, enterprise/OEM data-center memory, and data-center storage.
  • Mobile and Client: revenue was $11.52bn, with 87% gross margin and 86% operating margin. This matters because the upside is not only hyperscaler HBM; tight DRAM/NAND supply and high-value client/mobile products are also contributing.
  • Automotive and Embedded: revenue was $4.63bn, with 79% gross margin and 75% operating margin. Micron also cited 1-gamma LPDDR5 automotive readiness and first 1-gamma DDR5 samples to a robotaxi customer.

🟥 Strategic Customer Agreements — The New Memory-Cycle Variable

  • Take-or-pay structure: Micron’s 10-Q says it recently executed strategic customer agreements, including agreements after May 28, structured as take-or-pay commitments for specific volumes over multi-year terms.
  • Pricing guardrails: most agreements have either fixed pricing or minimum/maximum pricing; a minority are market-priced. That suggests Micron is trying to trade some peak-cycle optionality for more durable demand visibility.
  • Backlog disclosure is conservative: Micron said remaining performance obligations were about $5bn as of May 28, but that figure is based on minimum committed volumes/minimum pricing and excludes agreements without fixed pricing or price bands. So the disclosed RPO likely understates the full commercial importance of the agreements.
  • Investor implication: the key debate is whether these contracts structurally reduce memory-cycle volatility, or merely lock in strong economics during an AI-driven shortage. That distinction will matter when supply catches up.

⬛ Risks / Watch Items

  • Capex intensity: Micron spent $7.1bn net on capex in Q3 and $19.6bn over the first nine months of fiscal 2026. The company is investing hard into the AI-memory window; the risk is overbuilding if demand visibility proves less durable than the contracts imply.
  • Customer concentration: the HBM4 language points to a lead customer platform, and the 10-Q includes customer-concentration risk tags. That is normal in AI accelerators, but it means platform timing and allocation decisions can matter a lot.
  • Execution risk: HBM4 high-volume shipments, HBM4E calendar-2027 production, 1-gamma DRAM ramps, and advanced die-stacked RDIMMs all require clean manufacturing execution.
  • Policy/geography risk: Micron’s 10-Q forward-looking risk language calls out tariffs, trade regulation, global operations, CHIPS Act incentives, and China/CAC context. Those are not the main earnings headline, but they remain background risk for a global memory supplier.

📌 Bottom Line for Master Jin

  • This is an AI infrastructure earnings print, not just a memory-cycle rebound. Revenue, margins, FCF, and guidance all say AI memory has moved from “beneficiary” to “scarce platform input.”
  • HBM and contracted demand are the thesis. HBM4 shipments plus take-or-pay strategic customer agreements are the pieces that make Micron’s earnings feel more durable than a normal DRAM upswing — but they also raise customer/platform concentration risk.
  • The thing to watch next is whether supply discipline survives success. If Micron and peers add too much capacity into an AI boom, today’s extraordinary margins can normalize fast; if customer commitments stay firm, Micron may have a stronger floor than prior memory cycles.
