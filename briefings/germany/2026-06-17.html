🇩🇪 Germany Economy

Germany has edged back into growth, but the recovery is still fragile: exports and public spending are helping while energy prices, weak orders, and soft labor demand keep the near-term bias cautious.

Headline

  • Real GDP rose 0.3% q/q in Q1 2026 and 0.5% y/y price-adjusted, with Destatis pointing to a strong export contribution at the start of the year.
  • May inflation cooled to 2.6% CPI y/y; HICP inflation was 2.7% y/y, while core CPI excluding food and energy stood at 2.5%.
  • The Bundesbank expects the recovery to remain subdued at first, then pick up, with calendar-adjusted real GDP forecast at 0.6% in 2026 and 1.3% in 2027.

Industrial / trade signal

  • Industrial production rose 0.4% m/m in April, but was still 0.5% lower y/y; construction and chemicals helped, while automotive production fell 4.7% m/m.
  • Manufacturing orders fell 3.8% m/m in April after March’s rebound; autos, electrical equipment, and machinery were the main drags.
  • Exports improved 0.9% m/m in April to €136.6B, but imports rose faster at 1.2% m/m; the adjusted trade surplus narrowed to €14.5B.
  • The truck toll mileage index rose 1.6% m/m and 1.8% y/y in May, a better high-frequency signal for goods movement than the weaker orders print.

Labor & consumer

  • Registered unemployment fell by 58,000 in May to 2.95M, with the unemployment rate down to 6.3%; the Federal Employment Agency warned the spring upturn still lacked momentum.
  • Labor demand remains low but stable: registered vacancies stood at 643,000, and the BA-X labor demand index rose one point to 103.
  • Business sentiment is stabilizing from a weak base: the ifo Business Climate Index rose to 84.9 in May from 84.5 in April, with expectations still subdued at 83.8.

Outlook

  • Near-term bias: fragile stabilization. Q1 GDP and April exports improved, but manufacturing orders and autos still signal that the rebound is not yet broad-based.
  • Watch energy and external demand: energy was still the clearest inflation pressure in May, while exports to the United States were 12.9% lower y/y and China-bound exports fell 3.5% m/m in April.
  • Upside comes from fiscal support, defense/infrastructure spending, and eventual residential construction recovery; downside comes from energy volatility, tariffs/geopolitics, and weak China demand.
